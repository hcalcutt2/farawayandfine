About Geirangerfjord

Geirangerfjord is a 15-kilometre branch of the Sunnylvsfjord in the Møre og Romsdal county of western Norway. Designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2005 alongside the Nærøyfjord, it is widely considered the most dramatic fjord in Europe — with walls rising up to 1,400 metres from sea level and a succession of waterfalls fed by glacial snowmelt. The Seven Sisters waterfall, comprising seven separate streams falling side by side, is accessible only on foot from the water.

Faraway & Fine operates the MV Nordlys exclusively in the Geirangerfjord system between May and September. The vessel's shallow draft allows access to the innermost reaches of the fjord, including the abandoned farms of Skageflå and Knivsflå, perched on near-vertical cliff faces 250 metres above the water and reachable only by hiking from the fjord below.

The Nærøyfjord, included in all Faraway & Fine Geirangerfjord itineraries, is the narrowest fjord in Europe at just 250 metres at its narrowest point. The walls on both sides rise sheer from the water and the light at the base of the fjord has a quality unlike anywhere else in Norway — particularly in the early morning, when mist clings to the upper slopes and the surface of the water is completely still.

When to Go

May

Snow still on peaks. Cold but clear. Waterfalls at maximum flow.

Jun–Jul

Midnight sun. Long warm evenings. Peak season — busier at Geiranger village.

Aug

Best weather. Warmest swimming. Some tourist traffic on main fjord.

Sep

Autumn colour beginning. Quieter. First chance of northern lights.

Practical Information

The nearest airport is Ålesund Vigra (AES), approximately 85 kilometres by road from Geiranger. Faraway & Fine arranges all transfers from Ålesund by private car. Alternatively, guests may fly into Bergen Airport (BGO) and connect by helicopter — a 45-minute flight over the fjord landscape that Faraway & Fine can arrange on request. The MV Nordlys departs from and returns to Ålesund harbour.
