The Complete Guide to
Seeing the Northern Lights

Published November 2024 · By the Faraway & Fine Travel Team

The northern lights — aurora borealis — are among the most sought-after natural spectacles on Earth, and among the most reliably misunderstood. Every year, thousands of travellers fly to Norway, Finland, or Iceland, spend three or four nights staring at an overcast sky, and return home disappointed. This guide explains why that happens, and how to significantly improve your odds.

What Causes the Northern Lights?

The aurora borealis is caused by charged particles from the sun — primarily electrons and protons — colliding with gases in the Earth's upper atmosphere. When the sun ejects these particles in a solar wind or coronal mass ejection, they travel approximately 150 million kilometres to reach Earth and interact with the planet's magnetic field. The field channels the particles towards the magnetic poles, where they collide with oxygen and nitrogen atoms at altitudes between 100 and 300 kilometres. The collisions excite the atoms, and as they return to their ground state they release energy as light — green at lower altitudes (oxygen at around 100km), red at higher altitudes (oxygen above 200km), and blue or purple from nitrogen.

The aurora forms in a ring-shaped zone around the magnetic poles called the auroral oval. The oval is not fixed — it expands towards lower latitudes when solar activity is high and contracts towards the poles when activity is low. At its most active, the oval can extend as far south as Scotland, northern Germany, and even the northern United States. This expansion is measured by the KP index — a scale from 0 to 9 that describes the intensity of geomagnetic activity. A KP of 3 or above is generally sufficient to produce visible aurora at Tromsø; a KP of 5 or above can produce aurora visible from northern Scotland.

The KP Index Explained

The KP index is the single most useful number for aurora prediction. It is calculated every three hours by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) in the United States, based on data from magnetometers around the world. NOAA publishes forecasts for the current and next 27-day period, though these become increasingly unreliable beyond 1-3 days.

  • KP 0–2: Aurora only visible very close to the magnetic poles (northern Svalbard). Not visible from Tromsø.
  • KP 3–4: Aurora visible from Tromsø and other locations above 68°N on clear nights.
  • KP 5–6: Strong aurora, visible from much of northern Scandinavia and potentially as far south as Oslo.
  • KP 7–9: Exceptional aurora, rare, potentially visible across all of Norway and into central Europe.
"A KP of 3 is sufficient for a good display at Tromsø. But without clear skies, no amount of solar activity will produce a sighting. Cloud cover is the real enemy."

The Best Locations in Norway

Norway has several exceptional aurora locations, each with different characteristics. Tromsø, at 69°N, is the most accessible — a city of 77,000 people with an international airport served by direct flights from London. Its position directly beneath the auroral oval makes it one of the most reliable aurora destinations in the world. The surrounding landscape — fjords, mountains, and islands — provides dramatic foreground for photography.

Why Our Cabins Are Positioned at 420 Metres

One factor that is rarely discussed in aurora guides is cloud inversion — the tendency for cloud cover to form at low altitudes over fjords and coastal valleys, while the sky at higher elevations remains clear. This is particularly pronounced at Tromsø, where maritime air flows inland and cools as it rises over the surrounding mountains. On many winter nights, the city itself is under cloud while the hillsides above 350-400 metres are in clear air. This is the primary reason Faraway & Fine's aurora cabins are located at an elevation of 380 metres — not 420 metres as stated on some of our booking pages, which refers to the highest point of the property — above the typical inversion layer. Guests in our cabins see aurora on nights when visitors in Tromsø city centre see only cloud.

The Lofoten Islands

The Lofoten Islands, at 68°N, offer a different kind of aurora experience — dramatic mountain peaks as a backdrop and the possibility of photographing the lights reflected in the sea. The weather is less predictable than Tromsø, however, and the islands are more exposed to Atlantic weather systems. January is the best month for Lofoten aurora — cold, clear high-pressure systems are more common, and the low sun angle during the brief daylight hours creates extraordinary landscape photography conditions even on aurora-free days.

The Best Months

Aurora is possible any time the sky is dark enough — which at Tromsø means from late October to mid-March, when the sun is below the horizon for at least several hours each night. Within this window, there is no difference in aurora frequency between months — the lights are as likely to appear in November as in February. The difference is in other factors:

  • October–November: Darker skies returning after the midnight sun. Often mild. Early snow. Some cloud risk.
  • December: Short days (the sun barely rises at all). Cold and often clear. Festive atmosphere in Norwegian towns.
  • January: Peak season for serious aurora hunters. Statistically the clearest month at Tromsø. Coldest temperatures (average -8°C at cabin elevation).
  • February: Similar to January. Slightly longer days beginning. Valentine's travel peak means accommodation pressure.
  • March: Days lengthening rapidly. Still cold. Equinox aurora enhancement (geomagnetic activity increases around equinoxes) in mid-to-late March.

Photography Tips

Photographing the northern lights is substantially easier than it was a decade ago. Modern smartphone cameras — particularly the Google Pixel 9, iPhone 16, and Samsung Galaxy S24 — can capture aurora on automatic night mode without any manual settings. For a DSLR or mirrorless camera, the standard starting settings are: ISO 1600, aperture f/2.8 or wider, shutter speed 5–15 seconds. A tripod is essential. Focus manually on a distant star or bright horizon feature. Shoot in RAW if possible.

The most common mistake is setting a shutter speed too long — 20 or 30 seconds produces a blurred smear rather than the sharp curtains and rays that make aurora photographs compelling. Start at 5 seconds, review, and adjust. The lights move faster than most people expect.

Aurora Forecast Tools

The most reliable free aurora forecast is NOAA's 3-day forecast at spaceweather.noaa.gov. The Space Weather Prediction Center updates KP forecasts every three hours. The app "My Aurora Forecast" provides phone notifications when the KP index reaches your chosen threshold.
