New Nordic Food:
What It Is and
Where to Find It

Published September 2024

In 2004, twelve chefs signed a manifesto that would change the way the world thought about food. The New Nordic Manifesto, drafted at a symposium in Copenhagen and signed by chefs from Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Finland, Iceland, and the Faroe Islands, set out ten principles for a new approach to Northern European cooking — one rooted in local ingredients, traditional techniques, and the specific landscapes and climates of the Nordic region.

Twenty years later, the movement those twelve chefs started has been credited with influencing restaurant cooking worldwide, producing dozens of Michelin stars across Scandinavia, and transforming Copenhagen into one of the world's foremost food cities. Here is what the New Nordic movement actually means, and where to find the best examples of it in 2024.

The Ten Principles

The New Nordic Manifesto lists ten core principles. The most important, for understanding what the cooking actually tastes like, are:

  • PurityIngredients should be treated with restraint. The natural flavour of a scallop, a piece of venison, or a root vegetable should be the point of the dish, not masked by cream, butter, or complex sauces.
  • SeasonalityThe menu should reflect what is available right now in the Nordic landscape — which means almost no tomatoes or asparagus in February, but extraordinary produce in August and September.
  • ForagingWild plants, mushrooms, berries, and herbs gathered from forests, coastlines, and hedgerows should feature prominently. A New Nordic chef will typically forage personally, or work with a dedicated forager.
  • FermentationTraditional Nordic preservation techniques — lacto-fermentation, salt-curing, smoking, pickling — are used not only to preserve ingredients but as flavour-building processes in their own right.
  • LocalityIngredients should come from within the Nordic region wherever possible. This is not absolute — coffee and spices are permitted — but the dominant flavours of a dish should be Nordic.

Copenhagen: The Capital of New Nordic

Copenhagen has 24 Michelin-starred restaurants — more per capita than Paris. Many of the most celebrated are explicitly New Nordic in their philosophy. Faraway & Fine's Copenhagen programme includes a private chef's table dinner at one of the city's Michelin-recognised restaurants, arranged personally by our Cultural Director. The specific restaurant changes based on what is available and exceptional at the time of each visit — we do not name specific restaurants in our marketing materials because the landscape changes season by season and we prefer to recommend what is genuinely best rather than what appeared in a guide published two years ago.

The Torvehallerne covered market in central Copenhagen is perhaps the best single place to understand New Nordic ingredients. Opened in 2011, it brings together around 60 food stalls and shops under two glass-roofed halls near Nørreport station. A morning visit here with Faraway & Fine's Cultural Director — who knows many of the vendors personally — is included in the Copenhagen programme.

Sweden: Foraging and Fermentation

The New Nordic influence in Sweden is perhaps most visible in the foraging culture that has always been part of Swedish life — the principle of allemansrätten (every person's right) grants all Swedes the legal right to walk, camp, and forage on any land, private or public. Swedish chefs have built on this tradition with particular sophistication. The Sörmland region south of Stockholm, where Faraway & Fine conducts guided foraging expeditions on the Swedish Gastronomy Journey, is one of the richest foraging landscapes in Scandinavia — mixed forest with oak, birch, and pine providing chanterelles, porcini, cloudberries, lingonberries, and wild garlic depending on the season.

The Faraway & Fine Sweden New Nordic Gastronomy Journey runs for 7 nights and includes the Sörmland foraging expedition, a private chef's table in Gothenburg, and aquavit tastings in Malmö. Prices start from £5,800 per person based on two guests.
