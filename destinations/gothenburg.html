About Gothenburg

Gothenburg (Göteborg in Swedish) is Sweden's second-largest city and principal port, situated at the mouth of the Göta älv river on the country's west coast. Founded by King Gustav II Adolf in 1621, it was designed by Dutch engineers and retains a network of canals in the city centre. The Haga district — Gothenburg's oldest surviving neighbourhood — is a grid of 19th-century wooden houses, independent cafés, and craft shops on a hillside above the city centre.

Gothenburg has the highest concentration of Michelin-starred restaurants per capita of any Scandinavian city outside Copenhagen, and is widely considered the spiritual home of West Swedish seafood cooking. The Gothenburg Museum of Art, opened in 1923 at the head of Avenyn (the city's main boulevard), houses the largest collection of Nordic art outside of Scandinavia's national museums, including significant works by Carl Larsson, Anders Zorn, and Edvard Munch.

The Bohuslän coast stretching north of Gothenburg is among the most distinctive landscapes in Scandinavia — a labyrinth of low granite islands, tidal channels, and fishing villages that has been inhabited continuously since the Stone Age. UNESCO-listed rock carvings at Tanum, made between 1700 and 500 BC, depict ships, warriors, and animals across dozens of exposed rock faces. The coast is accessible only by sea for much of its length.
