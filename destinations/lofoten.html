About the Lofoten Islands

The Lofoten archipelago lies within the Arctic Circle off the northwest coast of Norway, in the Nordland county. It consists of six main islands — Austvågøy, Gimsøya, Vestvågøy, Flakstadøy, Moskenesøy, and Værøy — connected by bridges and tunnels and running roughly southwest from the mainland for approximately 160 kilometres. The islands are characterised by dramatic mountain peaks rising almost vertically from the sea, fjords cutting deep inland, and tiny fishing villages (known as fiskevær) clustered at the base of the cliffs.

January is the optimal month for a Lofoten winter expedition. The tourist season does not begin until February at the earliest, meaning the beaches, trails, and villages are effectively empty. The Lofoten Islands also have one of the mildest winter climates in the Arctic, owing to the Gulf Stream — temperatures in January average around -2°C, far warmer than comparable latitudes elsewhere in the world. The days are short (the sun rises around 10am and sets around 2pm) but the light during those hours, filtered through Arctic air and reflecting off snow-covered peaks, is extraordinary.

The Lofoten Islands are home to one of the largest populations of white-tailed sea eagles in Europe. These birds — with a wingspan of up to 2.5 metres — are regularly visible from the water, particularly around the fish drying racks (hjell) that still line the harbours of Svolvær, Henningsvær, and Reine. Faraway & Fine's Lofoten programme includes a dedicated sea eagle safari by RIB, led by a local ornithologist with 20 years of experience on the archipelago.

A Note on Rorbuer Accommodation

The rorbuer (singular: rorbu) is the traditional dwelling of the seasonal cod fishermen who came to Lofoten from across Norway each winter. Originally wooden shacks built on stilts over the water's edge, they have been converted in recent decades into characterful accommodation — retaining the traditional red-painted exterior and the distinctive smell of salt air and pine, but fitted with modern heating, kitchens, and bathrooms. Faraway & Fine uses two private rorbuer complexes in different parts of the archipelago, allowing guests to experience both the inner and outer islands during their stay.
